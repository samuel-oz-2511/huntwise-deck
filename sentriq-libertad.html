01 / Technology and Scientific Background

Built from the inside. Impossible to replicate from outside.

Sentriq's detection engine is grounded in 18 months of empirical research conducted from inside the recruiting industry. Samuel operated as a professional recruiter inside Greenhouse, Comeet, and Workday -- a legitimate role that provided direct access to the full hiring attack surface. The behavioral corpus was built by systematically measuring how application content, keyword placement, formatting, and submission timing influenced progression through real hiring pipelines across dozens of roles, companies, and hiring contexts. ATS filter behavior is not secret -- it is documented, testable, and exploitable by anyone who researches it correctly. The research was methodical, not covert. 30+ documented experiments. 73,500 lines of empirical detection code. The corpus is not synthetic. It was built by running the offense from inside the system, then reverse-engineering the defense.

The system operates across three detection layers:

1
Submission Metadata Analysis
Timing patterns, device fingerprinting, behavioral velocity, automation signatures. Detects coordinated mass-application campaigns and bot-assisted submissions.
2
Contextual Authenticity Challenge
Multi-modal verification layer that adapts per role, company, and job context. Depending on what the context demands, it deploys written knowledge challenges, LLM-conducted live phone calls to verify real-time subject knowledge, accent and speech pattern analysis, or behavioral response profiling. LLM-generated text detection and cross-application similarity scoring also operate within this layer as passive signals. A candidate who memorized an AI-fabricated profile cannot pass a live LLM interrogation on the role's actual technical context. There is no training set an adversary can prepare against a challenge that changes with every application.
3
Identity-Application Coherence
Cross-references application content against verifiable signals to detect proxy submissions, stolen credential usage, and adversarial identity-skill mismatches.
The national security dimension: DPRK operatives have successfully infiltrated US defense contractors, technology companies, and financial institutions by passing standard identity verification. They use real stolen SSNs, professional photos, and polished AI-generated CVs. Identity checks confirm the person is real. Sentriq answers a different question: is this application real? That gap is the attack vector. We close it.

On evasion resistance: A sophisticated adversary who knows Sentriq exists cannot simply adapt around it. The detection is not rule-based -- it is behavioral. The corpus encodes 18 months of what legitimate hiring activity looks like across thousands of real submissions. An adversary attempting to mimic legitimate behavior must first have access to that baseline, which they do not. Evading Layer 1 increases anomaly signals in Layer 2. Evading Layer 2 increases exposure in Layer 3. The multi-layer architecture means partial evasion degrades the adversary's cover rather than restoring it. The corpus also grows with every enterprise deployment -- the baseline becomes harder to spoof over time, not easier.

Technical approach and intended validation: The detection engine uses a hybrid architecture: behavioral corpus analysis (Layer 1), a contextual authenticity challenge layer (Layer 2), and coherence analysis (Layer 3). Layer 2 is specifically designed to resist AI replication -- it generates a dynamic, contextual question set derived from the specific job, company, and role. The question set changes with every application. A real, qualified human answers it correctly from genuine context. An AI fabricating a profile has no stable training set to memorize. The combination of corpus baseline and contextual challenge is what makes the system resistant to adversarial adaptation over time.

Formal validation metrics -- precision, recall, false positive rate, and time-to-signal per application -- are among the primary data points Sentriq will gather under LOI with design partners WalkMe (SAP), Wells Fargo, and HSBC. Current benchmarks come from 18 months of internal corpus research and attack simulation. Those numbers will be produced within 60-90 days of first enterprise integration, and measuring them is a core deliverable of every design partner agreement.

On intellectual property: Patent strategy will develop as the product architecture solidifies. Our intent is to pursue protection on the behavioral corpus methodology and the Layer 2 contextual challenge framework. At this stage, what we can offer Libertad is first-mover access to the corpus and algorithms as they are built -- before the architecture is locked and before commercial contracts define what can be shared.

03 / Competitive Differentiation and Added Value

The only product purpose-built for the pre-submission layer.

Every existing security tool in the hiring funnel operates downstream of the problem:

Identity Verification (Persona, Checkr)
Confirms the person is real. Does not evaluate whether the application is authentic. DPRK operatives pass identity checks using real stolen SSNs.
Background Checks
Operate post-hire. The adversarial actor is already inside the organization before the check runs.
ATS Fraud Filters
Designed for spam volume, not adversarial sophistication. No behavioral baseline. No cross-application intelligence.
Sentriq
Pre-submission layer. Platform-agnostic. Upstream of every ATS, every background check, every identity check. The only product that surfaces a trust signal before the application enters any system.

Added value to the market: Sentriq introduces a detection layer that has never existed in any hiring infrastructure. The product does not replace any existing tool -- it operates upstream of all of them, before the application enters any system. Every enterprise that deploys Sentriq gains a behavioral baseline across its hiring pipelines. Every deployment makes the corpus denser. The cumulative effect is a network that becomes more accurate as adoption grows -- a structural moat no competitor can acquire with capital alone, because the corpus requires operating the offense at scale to build.

On replication by intelligence-adjacent firms: The question worth addressing directly: could a better-resourced intelligence firm -- NSO, Toka, or a state-backed lab -- simply build this? The answer is specific to this use case. Detecting adversarial hiring applications requires deep immersion in the commercial hiring funnel: how HR teams configure ATS platforms, how the CISO and HR co-own purchasing decisions, how async challenge systems behave in practice, and what a legitimate application looks like across industries and seniority levels at scale. That knowledge is not an intelligence capability. It is a commercial GTM capability embedded in the product. NSO does not know what a Greenhouse webhook looks like. They do not know how a recruiter reviews an async video screen. Building Layer 2 specifically -- the contextual challenge layer -- requires the kind of HR domain expertise that only exists inside commercial enterprise hiring operations. The moat is not a technical secret. It is operational, built by running the offense from inside the system being defended.

Israeli competitive landscape: There are no Israeli companies currently operating at the pre-submission application integrity layer. Israeli HR-tech focuses on talent matching and recruitment automation. Israeli cyber operates in network security, endpoint, and threat intelligence -- none of which addresses the hiring attack surface. The closest adjacent players are Israeli background verification services, which operate post-hire. The category does not exist in Israel yet. That is the opening.

Additionally: the value to a commercial enterprise is not limited to organizations facing state-level attacks. Any company running hiring pipelines without an application integrity layer is making systematic errors in talent selection -- even if a deliberate adversarial attempt happens once every five years. The ROI for a CISO or HR team is measured against the cost of a single wrong hire in a sensitive role, which regularly exceeds the annual Sentriq contract by an order of magnitude. The threat spectrum runs from opportunistic AI-generated applications all the way to state-sponsored insertion. Sentriq addresses all of it with the same layer.
