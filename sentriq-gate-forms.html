01 — Four Gate Forms
Sentriq
Sentriq
Form A
The Lintel
The arch removed. Two pillars, one horizontal bar. Every element is a pure rectangle — nothing that degrades at small size. The flat lintel reads as weight and permanence where an arch reads as movement. References the Greek portico and the Japanese torii simultaneously. The gate defined by what flanks the opening, not what curves above it.
All fills No curves Scales to 12px
Sentriq
Sentriq
Form B
The Drawn Gate
One continuous stroke: left pillar, arch, right pillar. No fills, no mass — the entire structure is a single unbroken line. The beacon is the only solid element and becomes the visual anchor. Reads like a blueprint or technical drawing. As the brand matures, line-art marks carry more credibility than filled shapes. This form scales from a favicon to a billboard without losing character.
Single path All strokes Beacon-first
Sentriq
Sentriq
Form C
The Bracket
No arch. Two facing brackets define the gate purely through negative space — the opening is not drawn, only implied by what surrounds it. The form reads as embrace and containment simultaneously. Four rectangles. No curve anywhere. The most abstract and the most versatile: it carries no architectural period, no cultural reference, no historical weight. It just says: here is a threshold, something watches it.
Four rects No arch Negative space gate
Sentriq
Sentriq
Form D
The Peaked
A pointed arch instead of a Roman semicircle. The peak is directional — it drives upward, like a fortress gate or a citadel entrance. The arch is a stroke, the pillars are fills: the structure and the opening are visually distinct. The beacon floats above the apex rather than sitting in it. More tension in the form, appropriate for defense sector and CISO audiences where the security posture needs to feel active, not passive.
Pointed arch Mixed fills + strokes Defense energy
