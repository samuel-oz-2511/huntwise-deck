Section 1

What Sentriq does (in plain language)

Sentriq sits between a job applicant and the employer's hiring system. When someone submits a job application, Sentriq passively observes behavioral signals during that process: how they type, whether they copy-paste, how long they spend on each field. This is Layer 1.

If anomalies are detected, the candidate is redirected to a short async challenge hosted by Sentriq. The challenge is generated by AI and scored behaviorally, but a Sentriq analyst reviews and confirms the signal before anything is issued. This is Layer 2.

If the score is flagged, the analyst review is completed before the result reaches the employer's recruiter. The employer always makes the final hiring decision. This is Layer 3.

Core legal design principle
Sentriq is a neutral third party processor. It processes behavioral data on behalf of each employer. Candidates flagged beyond the passive layer interact directly with Sentriq's own interface under Sentriq's own privacy notice. The employer does not collect behavioral data. This distinction is legally critical and intentional.

What Sentriq does NOT do

It does not verify identity. It does not make hiring decisions. It does not auto-reject candidates. Cleared candidate data is deleted immediately once passed to the ATS. Flagged candidate data is isolated per employer and deleted on a defined schedule. Only confirmed proxy fraud profiles, verified by CISO evidence and a second Sentriq internal review, may enter the cross-company graph.

Biometric classification: confirmed defensible
The position that Sentriq's keystroke data is not biometric was confirmed as defensible in the April 2026 consultation. The data is never used to uniquely identify a person. The product is architecturally blocked from identity verification use cases. A formal written legal opinion confirming this classification is pending. Internal guidance: do not use the word "biometric" in customer conversations or marketing copy. Do not draw attention to the question.
Voice data vs. voiceprint data
Voice data captured during a challenge is not biometric. Voiceprint data (a mathematical template extracted from audio and used to identify a specific person across interactions) is biometric and triggers GDPR Art. 9, BIPA, and CCPA biometric provisions. Sentriq's voice analysis must remain at the behavioral pattern level and never create an identity template. This is an engineering constraint, not just a legal position.
Terminology rule: do not use "fingerprint"
"Device fingerprinting" is a legally and technically specific term referring to cross-site user tracking. It triggers specific regulatory obligations and is often prohibited by browser terms of service. Sentriq does not do device fingerprinting. Use "fraud profile," "behavioral signal," or "fraud identifier" in all customer conversations, contracts, and external documentation.

1. Is keystroke data biometric under GDPR Art. 9 or BIPA?
Position: no. Keystroke timing patterns are pseudonymous behavioral metadata used to detect anomalous patterns, never to create a biometric identifier for a specific individual. The product is architecturally blocked from identity verification use cases. Counsel confirmed this position is defensible in the April 2026 consultation. Pending: written legal opinion from privacy counsel.
Architecture decided
2. What is the lawful basis for processing under GDPR Article 6?
Legitimate Interest (Art. 6(1)(f)) is the primary basis. The legitimate interest is fraud prevention and workforce security. GDPR Recital 47 explicitly names fraud prevention as valid. Counsel confirmed consent is not the right basis in employment contexts (Recital 43). A Legitimate Interest Assessment (LIA) is required to document the balancing test formally. Pending: LIA drafted by counsel.
Counsel confirmed
3. Is candidate consent valid in an employment context?
GDPR consent in employment contexts is generally not freely given due to the employer-candidate power imbalance. Sentriq's neutral third party structure solves this: the candidate interacts with Sentriq's interface under Sentriq's own privacy notice before any data is collected, independent of the employer. Counsel confirmed this structure is legally sound and that legitimate interest, not consent, is the correct primary basis. Pending: privacy notice reviewed by counsel.
Strategy in development
4. Can Sentriq legally build a cross-company fraud signal?
This question has two distinct answers depending on which part of the product is involved.

All threat layers below proxy fraud: Data stays in a closed universe per employer. Sentriq processes it as a processor on behalf of that employer only. No cross-company sharing occurs. No additional legal mechanism is required beyond the standard DPA.

Confirmed proxy fraud profiles only: Sentriq steps into a controller position over that specific profile. A confirmed proxy fraud profile enters the graph only after: (1) CISO at the originating employer confirms fraud with documented evidence, and (2) Sentriq's own analyst conducts a second internal review and confirms the classification. Counsel's proposed approach: add a specific clause to the standard DPA where each employer agrees that Sentriq may retain and share confirmed proxy fraud identifiers across the network. This makes the employer an informed co-controller for that narrow case. Counsel is finalizing the exact strategy with Sapir.

Pending: DPA fraud graph clause, confirmed strategy from counsel.
Mitigation in place
5. Illinois BIPA: biometric data law, one of the strictest in the world
Phase 1 (current): Illinois candidates are identified by IP and geo-fenced from the behavioral challenge. They go directly to Layer 3 human review. No behavioral data is collected. Phase 2 (pre-first US customer): Full BIPA compliance package: written policy, written release at challenge entry, data destruction schedule documented (90-day maximum retention), registered agent filing in Illinois. Pending: BIPA compliance package from US counsel.
Assessment in progress
6. EU AI Act: Annex III high-risk classification
Hiring AI is listed as high-risk under EU AI Act Annex III. Compliance deadline: August 2, 2026.

Exception angle under assessment: Counsel is evaluating whether Sentriq qualifies for an exception by framing the AI as a challenge generation tool (the AI writes the questions), with the human analyst as the actual decision point. If this framing holds, the high-risk classification does not apply and the product is significantly easier for EU customers to procure.

If high-risk classification applies: Required elements are human oversight mechanisms, technical documentation, candidate transparency, and a conformity assessment. Sentriq's Layer 3 human review directly satisfies the human oversight requirement. Compliance becomes a procurement advantage, not a burden, given the August 2026 deadline every EU enterprise faces.

Pending: formal EU AI Act classification assessment from counsel.
Counsel confirmed
7. Data controller allocation: who is responsible for what?
Sentriq is a data processor for the core product. It processes candidate behavioral data on behalf of each employer. Each employer is the data controller for their own candidates. This position was confirmed by counsel in the April 2026 consultation and replaces the previous version of this document, which incorrectly classified Sentriq as controller for challenge data.

Sentriq becomes an independent data controller only for confirmed proxy fraud profiles that enter the cross-company graph. This is a narrow, high-threshold case with a two-step confirmation process before any profile is added.

A standard DPA is required for every enterprise contract and must include a specific clause for the controller role Sentriq takes on confirmed fraud profiles.

Pending: standard DPA template drafted by counsel.

8. CCPA / CPRA (California)
Business-to-business exemption applies to the employer relationship. For California resident candidates, CCPA rights apply: right to know, right to delete, right to opt out of sale. Sentriq does not sell candidate data in the ordinary sense.

Open item on the fraud graph: Under CCPA, "sale" includes disclosing personal information in exchange for commercial benefit, not just direct payment. If Sentriq shares confirmed fraud signals across customers as part of a paid subscription, this may be classified as a "sale," creating an opt-out obligation. Counsel flagged this as an active issue. The redress mechanism in Section 5, Question B is the likely vehicle for any required opt-out. Counsel is assessing whether the fraud prevention context creates a carve-out.

Pending: CCPA "sale" classification assessment for the graph.
Counsel confirmed

Counsel confirmed Sentriq is not a data broker under CCPA. On FCRA: Sentriq's behavioral signal is a fraud detection signal, not a consumer report or background check. The employer makes all hiring decisions independently. This position was confirmed as reasonable in the April 2026 consultation. Section 3B

EU cookie and SDK consent: the passive layer problem

This issue was identified by counsel in the April 2026 consultation. It is not a data classification issue. It is a cookie consent issue triggered by the technology itself.

The issue
The passive tracking layer works by embedding Sentriq's SDK in the employer's application form page. Under EU ePrivacy rules (and GDPR), dropping a tracking SDK on a web page may require user consent before it activates. If a candidate in the EU does not consent, the passive layer cannot run for that candidate.

Jurisdiction breakdown

United Kingdom: A fraud prevention carve-out exists. Tracking strictly necessary for fraud detection may not require prior consent. This is the most favorable regime for Sentriq.

European Union: The fraud carve-out does not exist in the same form. Whether the SDK falls under "strictly necessary" (no consent required) or "analytics/performance" (consent required) depends on how each employer categorizes it in their cookie policy. Sentriq cannot make this decision for the employer, but must provide clear technical documentation to help them categorize it correctly.

Practical consequence: EU employers that use full cookie consent models may find a portion of candidates opt out of the passive layer. Those candidates proceed directly to Layer 2 or the ATS without passive behavioral data. This reduces coverage but does not break the product.

Approach
Sentriq provides each EU enterprise customer with technical documentation explaining what the SDK collects and why, enabling the customer to make an informed categorization decision for their cookie policy. Sentriq does not instruct the customer on how to categorize it. Both categorization outcomes are handled by the product architecture.

Action required: Ben to prepare a technical spec of the SDK data collection. Counsel to review the language before it is shared with any customer.
Section 4

Documents that need to be drafted

These are the legal deliverables required before the first enterprise contract can be signed. None have been formally issued. Drafting begins after seed funding closes, targeting completion within 6 to 8 weeks of closing.

1. Legal Opinion: Biometric Classification
Written opinion confirming that keystroke dynamics and behavioral timing data are not biometric identifiers under GDPR Article 9 or Illinois BIPA. The position was confirmed as defensible in the initial consultation. Formal sign-off pending.
2. Legitimate Interest Assessment (LIA)
EU-required document under GDPR formally documenting the three-part balancing test: (a) Sentriq's legitimate interest in fraud detection, (b) necessity of the processing, (c) balancing against candidate rights. Must be on file before processing begins in the EU.
3. Candidate Privacy Notice
Plain-language notice shown to candidates before the behavioral challenge begins. Must cover: what is collected, why, how long it is kept, candidate rights (access, deletion, objection), data controller identity, and contact for data requests. Must comply with GDPR Articles 13-14.
4. Standard Data Processing Agreement (DPA) Template
The contract between Sentriq and each enterprise customer. Must define: Sentriq's processor role for the core product, controller/processor split, processing purposes and limits, data retention and deletion obligations, sub-processor disclosure, security requirements, breach notification timelines, GDPR Chapter V transfer mechanisms (Standard Contractual Clauses for non-EU transfers), and a specific clause governing Sentriq's independent controller role for confirmed proxy fraud profiles in the cross-company graph.
5. EU AI Act Classification Assessment
Formal written assessment of whether Sentriq falls under EU AI Act Annex III (high-risk) or qualifies for an exception based on the human-in-the-loop architecture. If high-risk: conformity assessment self-declaration. If exception: documented reasoning. Required before approaching any EU enterprise customer.
6. SDK Technical Documentation for EU Cookie Teams
Technical spec of what the passive layer SDK collects and why, written for enterprise cookie policy and procurement teams. Enables EU customers to make an informed categorization decision under ePrivacy rules. Ben produces the technical spec. Counsel reviews the language before any external distribution.
Section 5

Open questions that need counsel's view

These are the points where Sentriq does not yet have a finalized position and needs legal judgment before proceeding. Items A and B were open before the initial consultation. Items C through F were raised by counsel in the April 2026 session.

Needs counsel view
A. Does the neutral third party structure actually break the GDPR employment consent problem?
Sentriq is designed so that the candidate deals with Sentriq directly, not through the employer. The argument is that this removes the employer-candidate power imbalance that makes employment consent invalid under GDPR. Counsel's initial reaction was positive, but this has not been formally confirmed in writing. Would a regulator look through the structure and treat the interaction as employment-context consent regardless?
Needs counsel view
B. Candidate rights in the cross-company graph: deletion and redress
Two sub-questions:

Deletion: If a candidate requests deletion of their data under GDPR Article 17 or CCPA, Sentriq can delete their individual record. But behavioral patterns contribute to a shared fraud detection model. Does deleting the individual record satisfy the deletion requirement, or does the model itself need to be addressed?

Redress for false positives: Counsel recommended establishing a formal redress channel: a way for candidates who believe they have been incorrectly flagged to contact Sentriq and trigger a review. This serves two purposes: it is the correct thing to do if false positives occur, and it provides documented process that protects Sentriq legally if a challenge arises. The proposed mechanism is an email channel plus an internal review process that either clears the candidate or reaffirms the flag with documented reasoning. This channel also serves as the opt-out vehicle if the CCPA "sale" question in Item C requires one.
Needs counsel view
C. CCPA "sale" classification for the cross-company fraud graph
Under CCPA, "sale" includes disclosing personal information in exchange for commercial benefit, not just direct monetary payment. If Sentriq shares confirmed fraud signals across customers as part of a paid subscription, this may be classified as a "sale" of personal information, requiring California residents to have an opt-out right. Counsel is assessing whether the fraud prevention context creates a carve-out, or whether the DPA structure (employer agrees to graph sharing) changes the classification. If an opt-out is technically required, the redress channel in Item B above is the likely vehicle for it.
Needs counsel view
D. EU AI Act: does Sentriq qualify for an exception to the high-risk classification?
Counsel is exploring whether Sentriq can be positioned as falling outside Annex III by framing the AI as a challenge generation tool (the AI writes the questions) rather than a scoring or decision-making system (the human analyst makes the call). If this framing holds, Sentriq avoids the full high-risk compliance burden and is easier for EU customers to procure. If it does not hold, the full compliance package is required. Either outcome is workable. The exception angle is worth pursuing before accepting the high-risk classification as given.
Needs counsel view
E. EU passive layer: strictly necessary or analytics?
When an employer embeds Sentriq's SDK in their application form page, is that SDK "strictly necessary" for fraud prevention (no consent required under ePrivacy) or does it fall into a consent-required category? Sentriq cannot answer this categorization question for each customer. Counsel needs to produce guidance language that Sentriq can share with EU enterprise cookie policy teams to support them in making the correct decision. The answer may differ by jurisdiction within the EU.
Needs counsel view
F. Israeli privacy law: what does processing Israeli candidates require?
Israeli privacy law (Protection of Privacy Law 1981 and the PDPL amendments) is distinct from GDPR. Target early conversations include Israeli enterprises. What does processing behavioral data from Israeli candidates require under Israeli law? Is there an adequacy finding or do separate mechanisms need to be put in place?
