The Core Argument

Sentriq is enterprise infrastructure, not a consumer product. The seed round is the mechanism to achieve enterprise readiness - not the reward for having already achieved it. This is the standard model for Israeli cybersecurity companies that go on to raise Series A rounds.

Every successful Israeli cybersecurity infrastructure company follows the same capital sequence. The seed round is not a validation milestone - it is the unlock mechanism that makes the first enterprise customer possible.

SOC 2 requires 6-12 months and $50-150K depending on scope. That money is in the seed round. This is not a sequencing problem. It is the correct sequencing.

The Core Design Decision

Foundational Product Boundary

Sentriq collects behavioral metadata for fraud pattern detection and resulting risk scoring — no collection of biometric identifiers and no identity verification.

Sentriq’s processed data, including behavioral signals, are used for fraud pattern comparison and resulting risk scoring — not to identify who someone is. The system never asks “is this person who they claim to be?” — it asks “does this response pattern match a human expert working independently?” This distinction routes Sentriq around the highest-burden regulatory regime. It is documented in a written legal opinion.

Key Regulatory Questions Under Review

The questions below represent the regulatory landscape Sentriq is navigating. Positions are under active discussion with privacy counsel and DPO and are not yet finalized. The controller vs. processor determination is still being resolved and affects the answers across all questions. Final positions expected within the week.

1. Biometric Data Classification

Background: Biometric data is often treated as sensitive or special category data under applicable data protection laws. It often requires a higher justification for processing, conducting a data processing impact assessment (DPIA) by the data controller, and often also requires collecting the explicit written consent of the data subject. In the field of hiring, there is greater burden on the data controller to justify such processing even when consent is given, as there is an intrinsic imbalance of power between the candidate and the employer.

Definitions of biometric data in key go-to-market jurisdictions:

  • GDPR: “personal data resulting from specific technical processing relating to the physical, physiological or behavioural characteristics of a natural person, which allow or confirm the unique identification of that natural person, such as facial images or dactyloscopic data”
  • FTC: “data that depict or describe physical, biological, or behavioral traits, characteristics, or measurements of or relating to an identified or identifiable person’s body”
  • ICO: “Examples: keystroke analysis; handwritten signature analysis; gait analysis; gaze analysis (eye tracking)”
  • BIPA: “any information, regardless of how it is captured, converted, stored, or shared, based on an individual’s biometric identifier (retina or iris scan, fingerprint, voiceprint, scan of hand or face geometry) used to identify an individual”
  • CCPA: “includes, but is not limited to, imagery of the iris, retina, fingerprint, face, hand, palm, vein patterns, and voice recordings, from which an identifier template, such as a faceprint, a minutiae template, or a voiceprint, can be extracted, and keystroke patterns or rhythms, gait patterns or rhythms, and sleep, health, or exercise data that contain identifying information”

As a data controller: Sentriq’s position is that given the existing definitions noted above, the processed personal data does not fall under the definitions for biometric data under the prominent data protection laws noted above, as it is not used for the purpose of uniquely identifying the individual.

As a data processor: Sentriq would not be responsible for determining if the personal data processed falls under the definition of biometric data under applicable data protection laws and what legal basis should be relied on for such processing (including consent). Sentriq will be prepared to assist customers by contributing technological explanations regarding the data collected and processed — which can be used in Customer’s privacy policies and DPIAs. Sentriq’s product team will be prepared to create/facilitate additional pop-ups for Customers to display their privacy policy and/or collect consent or otherwise as requested by Customer.

Biometric data such as keystroke dynamics and typing behavior are not processed for unique identification. Written legal opinion confirms this classification. Product boundary: the system is architected to preclude identity verification use cases.

Preliminary analysis is examining Legitimate Interest and its applicability to fraud prevention in a hiring context, alongside alternative bases depending on the controller/processor determination. The approach to candidate-facing transparency — including the structure of any required notice, acknowledgment, or consent mechanism — is under review with privacy counsel and DPO. The product is designed to give candidates clear visibility before any data is collected, but the specific legal mechanism and its framing will be confirmed once the lawful basis and controller/processor questions are resolved.

4. Cross-Company Graph — Legal Architecture

The legal architecture for cross-employer signal sharing is under active review. The questions of what mechanism is required (consent, legitimate interest, or other basis), who acts as controller for the graph, and what information employers may receive — are all being worked through with counsel. The product is designed with these constraints in mind; the formal legal structure will be confirmed within the week.

5. Illinois BIPA and State-Level Biometric Laws

Exposure under Illinois BIPA and equivalent state-level biometric privacy laws is being assessed. Mitigation approaches are under discussion with counsel. The product architecture includes the ability to apply jurisdiction-specific handling; the specific compliance path will be confirmed as part of the broader legal opinion on data classification.

6. EU AI Act

Sentriq's potential classification under EU AI Act Annex III (recruitment and selection) and the resulting compliance obligations are under review with counsel. The product includes human oversight as a core layer, which is relevant to any high-risk classification. The full compliance posture and required conformity steps will be scoped as part of the ongoing legal review, ahead of the August 2026 deadline.

7. Data Retention

Appropriate retention periods for behavioral session data, risk scores, and graph signals are being defined in consultation with privacy counsel. The product architecture supports configurable and per-candidate deletion. Specific retention schedules and deletion obligations will be confirmed in the privacy notice and DPA template currently under preparation.

8. Controller vs. Processor

The determination of whether Sentriq operates as a data controller, data processor, or both — depending on the data type and processing activity — is the central open question currently under discussion with legal counsel. This determination affects the answers to all other questions above. A standard DPA template is being prepared to cover both scenarios and will be finalized as part of the current review.

9. FCRA and Sector-Specific Regulations

Applicability of the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) and sector-specific obligations (defense, OFCCP) are being assessed. The product's impact on hiring decisions and the required UI and disclosure obligations will be confirmed with US counsel as part of the first enterprise contract preparation.

Design Partner Program

What a design partner means at enterprise cyber seed stage, why it is the correct traction metric, and the current pipeline.

The Core Distinction

A design partner at this stage is not a user. Sentriq cannot deploy in production at any enterprise until SOC 2 Type I is complete. That is not a gap - it is the correct architecture for a product that sits inside enterprise hiring infrastructure. A design partner is a named enterprise that has confirmed the problem, evaluated Sentriq's approach, and committed to being among the first paying customers upon SOC 2 completion. No production access. No data shared pre-compliance. A directional commitment, typically documented as a signed LOI.

What Investor Diligence Looks Like at This Stage

When an investor requests a design partner reference call, they are not evaluating the product. They are asking the design partner three questions:

1. Do you have the problem?

Does your hiring pipeline face AI-generated applications, identity fraud, or credential misrepresentation at a scale that creates operational risk? For any enterprise running remote hiring at scale in 2026, the answer is yes.

2. Did you evaluate the approach?

Did a credible person at your company - CISO, Head of Talent, or VP Engineering - review Sentriq's architecture, research corpus, and product roadmap and find it technically credible? That is the diligence signal.

3. Would you buy it?

If Sentriq completes SOC 2 and delivers on the product roadmap, would you become a paying customer? A yes here - even directional - is the traction signal. Not a signed contract. A confirmed intent.

Why this is the right bar

Every Israeli enterprise cyber company at seed raised on this basis. Wiz, Axonius, Laminar, Apono - none had production enterprise deployments at seed. They had CISOs who said the problem was real and the approach was credible. That is the signal. Everything else is post-seed.
